Why would anyone hold a 2x leveraged ETF (正2) for the long term?

The answer isn't "great confidence in the market." It's "a system designed so confidence isn't required."

This system wasn't invented from scratch — it was assembled from three books.

---

## [*A Random Walk Down Wall Street*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Random_Walk_Down_Wall_Street) — Hold the Index

Burton Malkiel's core argument: markets are efficient, and most active managers can't beat the index over the long run. Instead of picking stocks, buy a fund that represents the entire market and let compounding do its work.

The takeaway: drop stock picking, hold index ETFs. It answers the question of *what* to hold.

---

## [*The Leveraged ETF Investing Method*](https://www.books.com.tw/products/0010953374) — Amplify Returns

Lin Cheng-hua's book analyzes the long-term behavior of 2x leveraged ETFs — specifically 00631L (元大台灣50正2): in trending bull markets, 2x products deliver significantly higher returns than their unleveraged counterparts, at the cost of greater volatility and deeper drawdowns.

The key insight: the longer the holding period, and with regular rebalancing, the less significant the volatility drag becomes. This provides the analytical basis for using 2x leverage in a long-term framework.

---

## [*The Psychology of Money*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Psychology_of_Money) — Leave Room for Error

The most important idea in Morgan Housel's *The Psychology of Money*: smart planning isn't about being more precise — it's about deliberately leaving room for error.

In practice: don't go all-in on leverage. Hold low-volatility assets (e.g. bond ETFs or cash) alongside 2x ETFs to cushion extreme scenarios, forming a "2x ETF + low-volatility asset" portfolio blend.

---

## The Execution Mechanism: Rebalancing

The three books provide the philosophy. Rebalancing provides the execution.

Resetting portfolio weights to the target allocation every quarter automatically achieves:
- After a big run-up: trim 2x exposure (sell some near the highs)
- After a big drop: add to 2x exposure (buy more near the lows)

No need to call the top or bottom. The mechanism itself does the "buy low, sell high."

---

## Why These Three Books Form a System

Each book answers a different question: what to hold, how to accelerate, how to manage risk. Rebalancing connects all three into a mechanism that can keep running on its own.

Remove any one piece and the system breaks down — holding the index without leverage leaves returns mediocre; adding leverage without a risk buffer means one crash could end the game; without rebalancing, there's no automatic mechanism to buy low and sell high. Together, the three books form an investment system that requires neither market prediction nor willpower.
