At the start of the year, my total trading capital was around NT$150,000. Today it's around NT$2,400,000.

Six months. Sixteen times.

This is the fastest growth I've seen in my trading life. I'm writing it down so I don't forget how it happened — and because there were a few key turning points I wish I'd understood much sooner.

## Starting with warrants — and getting stuck there

I started out trading primarily with warrants.

The fundamental problem with warrants is time decay. Even when you call the direction right, it's hard as a small-capital trader to size up properly. You see a great opportunity, but you can only take a small position. When the stock moves, your profit ends up completely out of proportion to your account size.

When you're wrong, warrants can go to zero faster than you can react. Add in the poor liquidity — you often slip badly on the way out.

The trap for small capital with warrants: when right, you can't size up; when wrong, losses come too fast.

## After switching to stock futures, I could finally scale positions

I gradually shifted my focus to individual stock futures (股票期貨, commonly called 股期 in Taiwan).

The biggest difference with stock futures is that they let you actually add to a position once you've confirmed the direction. Add when right, reduce or cut when wrong. It sounds simple, but I genuinely couldn't do it before. The old logic was: enter everything at once, smile if right, accept the loss if wrong.

Stock futures changed that logic entirely: open an initial position without going full size, add once the market confirms your direction, reduce first when wrong, then cut completely.

That's where the performance started to show up.

On the risk side, one fixed rule: **maintenance margin ratio must stay above 150% before market close**. On weak market days, raise that threshold and trim some positions. The goal is simple: keep capital flexible, always have ammunition for the next trade.

## Futures aren't dangerous — not understanding them is

Most people's first instinct about futures is "high leverage, blowups, stay away."

I used to think the same. But after using them, I understand: futures themselves aren't the scary part — it's people using them without understanding them that makes them dangerous.

If you understand what the maintenance ratio actually means (not "press it to the floor" — it's a warning threshold), that scaling in and out should follow conviction rather than emotion, and that capital flexibility matters more than today's returns, then futures are just a very useful tool.

Six months, NT$150,000 to NT$2,400,000. The thing I'm most grateful for isn't luck — it's finally finding an instrument where doing the right thing gets amplified, and doing the wrong thing gets controlled.
